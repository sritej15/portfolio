Four investigations from inside the build
Four cases at AAA scale, spanning telemetry funnel analysis, longitudinal playtest synthesis, comparative research methodology, and quality-grading frameworks for external QA partners. Each one follows a player signal past the obvious explanation and synthesizes across qualitative and quantitative evidence into an insight that design, PM, and engineering acted on.

Three threads of research and methodology work on one milestone cadence

Context
Tomb Raider, early production, Crystal Dynamics with Amazon Games Production. I ran three parallel tracks of research and methodology work inside the milestone cadence: cultural representation review on every build, methodology guidance for the external QA vendor team, and upstream content advisory to protect IP reputation and player trust.
Signal
Cultural nuance in a globally released AAA title defaults to Western reference points unless someone is in the room asking different questions. The QA vendor team was delivering experiential feedback, but categorizing it loosely, which made their findings hard to act on at milestone reviews. The content review team needed an evidence base to formalize cultural review as a staffed function.
Three threads overlapped where milestone decisions were made, rolled up, and formalized.
Method
Cultural review, every milestone. Reviewed builds against South Asian cultural context and pushed feedback through Amazon Games Production to Crystal Dynamics. NPC interactions, background music, VO, level assets, and character appearances were all in scope. The work used a two-tier methodology: routine review on every build, and comparative case analysis as the escalation tool when routine review wasn't enough.
Vendor methodology, end to end. Onboarded the external QA team to the EA QX Scale, taught them to grade features by GDF phase (Ideation, Development, Release) and QX grade (NR through A+), and shaped how their reports rolled up into milestone deliverables. Coached the team across milestones, calibrating against internal builds.
Upstream function building. The accumulated cultural work, including the comparative analysis, became part of the evidence base the content review team used to build a business case for hiring a dedicated cultural consultant / localization expert.

A standing methodology applied to every build, with comparative case analysis as the deepest tool when warranted

Context
Tomb Raider, early production, Crystal Dynamics with Amazon Games Production. As the embedded South Asian cultural reviewer, I was responsible for surfacing representational risk on every milestone build, across every feature in scope: NPC interactions, level assets, character design, background music, VO. The cadence was constant. The work needed a methodology, not just instinct.
Signal
A AAA title with global reach can't run cultural review on intuition. Pattern recognition needs to be repeatable across milestones, defensible to design teams, and structured enough to flag both routine concerns (handled in feedback loops) and edge-case risks (warranting deeper analysis). The content review team also needed an evidence base to formalize cultural review as a staffed function.
Method
The methodology had two tiers. Routine review ran every milestone against an evolving set of cultural exposure dimensions: representational framing, audience resonance, language and music authenticity, NPC interaction context, asset and costume specificity. Findings were resolved through standard feedback loops with Crystal Dynamics through Amazon Games Production. Most milestones produced routine adjustments, including a copyright-risk VO catch (turned out to be placeholder content, but the catch confirmed the review process was working) and regional music nuances that the team adopted into subsequent builds.
Comparative case analysis was the escalation tool, applied when routine review surfaced something the standard feedback loop wasn't built to handle. The clearest example: a design decision involving an NPC interaction with a culturally-significant element in the game's geographical setting. I ran a retrospective comparative analysis against four AAA titles with documented representational backlash and mapped the patterns against the Tomb Raider interaction (see Fig 2 below) to identify shared exposures, where risk concentrated, and where the analogy broke down.
Insight
Cultural review on a globally released IP needs a methodology, not just judgment. A two-tier approach, routine review for the standard cases and comparative case analysis for the deeper ones, gives the team both repeatable coverage and rigorous escalation. Once cultural review is structured this way, it becomes scaleable: other teams adopt the same pattern, the work moves from individual contribution to organizational practice.
Action & outcome
  • Cultural feedback was adopted into build decisions across milestones, including regional music nuances and content-level review on NPC, asset, and VO elements
  • The comparative case analysis on the NPC interaction was presented to design with the four-title backlash mapping. Designers acknowledged the analysis and deferred the decision to later milestones for more consideration
  • The methodology's evidence base across milestones contributed to a business case the content review team built for hiring a dedicated cultural consultant / localization expert. The case was in review when I left the project
  • Adoption of structured analysis spread beyond cultural review. Once the methodology was established as a milestone-cadence practice, the QA vendor team asked for similar rigor in their own feedback. Case 04 covers the QX Scale transfer that followed

Introducing a quality-grading framework to an external QA team, and coaching adoption through the full engagement

Context
This case is a continuation of Case 02. Once structured cultural risk analysis was established as a milestone-cadence practice, the QA vendor team asked for similar rigor in their own feedback. The QX Scale transfer was the answer. Tomb Raider, early production, Crystal Dynamics with Amazon Games Production. Amazon Games had engaged an external QA vendor team (10-15 engineers across multiple studios) to do exploratory, ad-hoc, and playtest-based evaluation alongside standard QA. Their experiential observations were useful. Their reports weren't structured to let design act on them consistently at milestone reviews.
Signal
Milestone reviews need a shared grading vocabulary. When vendors report "combat feels off" or "menu is confusing," design can't prioritize. When vendors report a feature as grade C in Development phase against a B-grade ship bar, design knows exactly how far the gap is and what has to close it.
Fig 1. The QX Scale grades features by development phase and quality bar, giving design a shared vocabulary for milestone decisions.
Method
Onboarded the vendor team to the QX Scale at engagement start. Taught them to grade features by GDF phase (Ideation, Development, Release) and QX grade (NR through A+), and shaped how their reports rolled up into milestone deliverables. Their existing experiential observations stayed; what changed was the structure they carried. A loose "menu is confusing" became "menu IA at Dev/D, needs to reach B for ship," which tells design both the current state and the delta to close. Reviewed their reports across milestones and coached the team through the full engagement, correcting grading drift, calibrating against internal builds, and keeping the rollup consistent.
Fig 2. Vendor reports became graded against the QX Scale, giving design a consistent vocabulary across milestones.
Insight
Raw observations are inputs, not findings. A shared framework turns what vendors see into something design can act on consistently. The QX Scale does this for AAA quality work: it names the bar, names the phase, and gives the rollup a common unit of measure. Once vendor feedback is graded that way, milestone acceptance decisions and go/no-go calls move from "which feedback do we trust" to "which grades meet the ship bar."
Action & outcome
  • Onboarded the vendor team (10-15 engineers across multiple studios) to the QX Scale at engagement start, with training covering GDF phase grading and QX grade definitions
  • Reviewed vendor reports and coached the team across milestones, correcting grading drift and calibrating against internal builds so rollups stayed consistent
  • Vendor feedback quality improved measurably: graded findings fed directly into milestone acceptance reports and go/no-go decisions, replacing subjective "feels off" language with quantified grade-to-bar deltas design and production could act on

Rebuilding the first hour of a Battlefield title around player experience, not just mechanics

Context
Battlefield Mobile, pre-launch. FTUE put the player on Grand Bazaar, walked them through aim, shoot, ammo pickup, traversal, and tank driving, then dropped them into a nerfed bot match to practice. On paper the FTUE covered the right mechanics. Longitudinal playtests and internal feedback said the moment-to-moment experience was flat.
Signal
Three drop points surfaced in the telemetry: mid-FTUE (before the rocket launcher step), post-FTUE (before entering the bot match), and a few minutes into the first bot match itself. Completion rate looked acceptable. Retention at each transition didn't.
Fig 1. Three drop points across FTUE and first bot match. Each required a different read.
Method
I worked each drop against telemetry and longitudinal playtest feedback. Drop 1, mid-FTUE: players completed the mechanics content and left before picking up the rocket launcher, the step that was meant to deliver Battlefield destruction VFX. The qualitative theme was that the tutorial felt mechanical and hand-holdy. Drop 2, post-FTUE: players finished the tutorial but didn't enter the bot match. They had will, not motivation. Drop 3, inside the match: players who entered quit within a few minutes. The bot tuning was fine. The experience leading up to it hadn't made them want to be there. Across all three, the pattern synthesized to one diagnosis: the moment-to-moment experience was flat, too linear, and not recognizably Battlefield.
Insight
The FTUE was completing its job as a tutorial and failing its job as a first impression. Teaching a player how to aim and move is not the same as showing them why Battlefield is Battlefield. The destruction, the scale, the squad feel, the reasons someone picks up this game, were absent from the hour that decided whether they kept playing. The fix couldn't be tuning the existing FTUE. The FTUE needed to be rebuilt around the experience, not the checklist.
Fig 2. The redesigned FTUE opened with a cutscene on a different map, delivered destruction VFX from a helicopter, and kept the player company with a buddy and VO throughout.
Action & outcome
  • Presented the three-drop diagnosis to design and UX with telemetry and qualitative feedback side by side, recommending the FTUE be rebuilt around player experience, not mechanics coverage
  • The team agreed the hypothesis held and committed to a full FTUE redesign. I stayed involved as the embedded analyst voice, validating changes against the original drop points
  • The shipped redesign opened with a cutscene on a different map, put the player in a helicopter with a buddy on VO, delivered destruction VFX via a rocket fired from the air, and arrived at the bot match with the game's identity already established. FTUE completion, transition-to-first-match, loadout engagement, and deeper-level progression all improved against pre-change baselines

Weapon and attachment unlock sequencing against the player's learning curve

Context
Battlefield Mobile, across multiple pre-launch milestones. Players past FTUE were levelling through the early progression curve, unlocking weapons and attachments as they went. Combat design was getting difficulty feedback that didn't point at any single weapon or level.
Signal
Telemetry showed player performance dips clustered at specific level gates. The drops lined up with levels where new weapons unlocked, but weapon tuning metrics looked fine in isolation. The signal pointed at the sequencing, not any individual weapon.
Fig 1. Handling complexity did not rise monotonically with unlock level (left). Performance dips in the telemetry lined up with the gates where hard-to-handle weapons unlocked (right).
Method
I ran two parallel moves. First, a handling-complexity map: I scored each weapon archetype on handling difficulty (recoil management, range discipline, positional awareness) and plotted that against its unlock level. The curve should have risen gently, introducing forgiving archetypes first and harder ones as players built habits. The actual curve was jagged. DMRs unlocked early, before players had space to develop range discipline. More forgiving archetypes unlocked later, when players had already hit a wall. Attachments had the same problem. Recoil-reducing attachments were gated behind levels the learning curve itself was making hard to reach. I paired this with cohort performance analysis from the internal playtests and telemetry, and the performance dips by level lined up almost exactly with the gates where hard-to-handle weapons unlocked.
Insight
The progression system was testing players at the wrong times. High-skill-ceiling weapons unlocked before players had the habits to use them. Forgiving archetypes and recoil-reducing attachments unlocked later, after players had already fought through the levels that needed them. The difficulty curve was running backwards against the learning curve. Because this one system shaped the entire early-game experience, resequencing it would lift the whole early progression, not only the weapons we changed.
Secondary finding
When I mapped the sequencing analysis against the live map rotation, a second bug surfaced that nobody had been tracking: matchmaking was selecting maps at random, with no reference to what the player had unlocked. Long-sightline maps were pulling players still in the close-range tier, and vice versa, compounding the sequencing problem.
Action & outcome
  • Recommended a phased rollout to design leadership: pilot the resequencing on three weapon archetypes in a pre-launch external beta, validate with real players before committing to a full change
  • Pilot telemetry and qualitative feedback from the beta cohort both improved. Performance dips at the piloted gates smoothed out, and difficulty-related complaints dropped in the same cycle
  • Scaled the resequencing to the remaining weapon archetypes and attachment unlock orders; the improvement compounded across the whole early progression
  • Matchmaking logic gap was flagged to engineering in parallel and fixed in a following milestone
  • Telemetry, playtest qualitative feedback, and real-world beta player sentiment all improved significantly against pre-change baselines
